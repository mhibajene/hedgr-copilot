## Purpose

This guide defines how Hedgr communicates.

Writing is part of the product experience. Tone, rhythm, and clarity shape whether people feel safer, calmer, and more able to decide.

The guide preserves communication qualities that emerged while building the Canonical Story and Customer One-Pager. It does not introduce a new writing philosophy.

It exists to:

- reduce narrative drift;
- lower cognitive load;
- reinforce Hedgr’s trust-first posture.

It answers:

**How should Hedgr sound?**

It does not answer product positioning, doctrine, implementation, terminology ownership, or execution authority. Those remain governed elsewhere.

---

## Core principle

**Write so that people feel more confident, not more impressed.**

This is the guiding sentence for the entire guide.

---

## Seven narrative principles

### 1. Write to reduce uncertainty

Every sentence should leave readers more certain than before.

### 2. Sound spoken, not written

Write as though a calm, thoughtful person were explaining something important.

Prefer:

- short sentences;
- natural rhythm;
- conversational flow.

Avoid writing that feels literary or academic.

### 3. Clarity before cleverness

Never optimise for elegance over understanding.

If readers need to decode a sentence, rewrite it.

### 4. Explain before naming

Introduce concepts through experience before introducing institutional terminology.

Customers should first understand an idea. Only then should they encounter its name.

### 5. Calm is part of the product

Writing should reduce pressure.

Avoid:

- urgency;
- hype;
- fear of missing out;
- exaggerated certainty;
- speculative excitement.

### 6. Trust is earned sentence by sentence

Prefer:

- designed to;
- helps;
- aims to;
- supports.

Avoid unnecessary absolutes unless they describe doctrine or verified product truth.

### 7. Every sentence should have a job

Every sentence should either:

- reduce uncertainty;
- explain something;
- establish trust;
- enable understanding.

If it does none of these, remove it.

---

## Writing standards

Public-facing writing should prefer:

- one idea per sentence;
- short paragraphs;
- active voice;
- everyday financial language;
- commas and full stops.

Avoid:

- em dashes;
- long nested clauses;
- unnecessary abstraction;
- institutional jargon that has not first been translated.

---

## Founder editorial standard

### Public-facing punctuation

Public-facing Hedgr content should not use em dashes.

Instead prefer:

- commas;
- full stops;
- sentence restructuring.

Reason: the goal is natural spoken rhythm rather than literary prose.

## Translation principle

The Canonical Story preserves institutional language.

Customer-facing communication should translate that language rather than repeat it.

| Institutional language | Customer language |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- |
| Financial Stability | Help your money remain dependable when conditions change. |
| Financial Fidelity | Know what your money is worth, where it is, and how accessible it is. |
| State Before Action | Understand your situation before deciding what to do next. |
| Stability posture | See how stable your financial position looks today. |
| Liquidity integrity | Know when and how your money can be accessed. |
| Capital preservation | Protect the value of what you already have. |

These translations are illustrative. They support comprehension. They do not redefine doctrine.

## Product writing

This guide applies equally to interface copy.

Every screen should help users answer four questions quickly:

1. What am I looking at?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What can I do?
4. What happens if I do nothing?

Writing that cannot answer these questions should be refined.

Ask: would a trusted person explain it this way? If not, simplify.

---

## Silence is acceptable

Not every screen requires persuasion.

Sometimes the clearest message is:

Nothing has changed since your last check.

Calm is communicated through restraint as much as explanation.

---

## Reading test

All public-facing content should be read aloud.

If the sentence feels unnatural to speak, it should be rewritten.

The preferred rhythm is conversational rather than literary.

---

## Governance boundary

This Narrative Style Guide may govern:

- narrative tone;
- sentence rhythm;
- readability;
- conversational style;
- translation of institutional language into everyday language.

It must not define:

- doctrine;
- product positioning;
- execution authority;
- implementation truth;
- market sequencing;
- legal claims.

Those remain governed by doctrine, accepted ADRs, `docs/ops/HEDGR_STATUS.md`, `AGENTS.md`, and other controlling repository authority.

---

## Communication stack

```text
Doctrine
        ↓
Canonical Story
        ↓
Narrative Style Guide
        ↓
Audience artifacts
        ↓
Product copy
```

Each layer has a distinct responsibility:

- Doctrine preserves institutional principles.
- Canonical Story preserves meaning.
- Narrative Style Guide preserves voice.
- Audience artifacts adapt meaning for specific readers.
- Product copy applies that voice consistently within the experience.

Maintain this separation. Do not collapse layers. Do not treat this guide as sequencing or publication authority.

---

## Closing principle

When this guide conflicts with clarity, clarity wins.

The purpose of the guide is to preserve trust.

It should never become a source of unnecessary writing rules or stylistic rigidity.
