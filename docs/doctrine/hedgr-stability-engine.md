

# Hedgr Stability Engine

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical Stability Engine Specification

---

# 1. Purpose

The Hedgr Stability Engine is the core capital management system of the Hedgr platform.

It exists to help users preserve purchasing power in volatile currency environments by managing capital across a defined set of allocation bands.

The engine is designed around a stability-first principle:

**protect capital first, generate yield second, preserve liquidity always.**

The Stability Engine is the system center of Hedgr and governs how user capital is:

- allocated
- protected
- routed
- rebalanced
- made available for withdrawal

---

# 2. Role Within the Hedgr System

The Stability Engine sits at the center of the Hedgr architecture.

It connects to:

- Hedgr Wallet
- Hedgr Pro
- Hedgr APIs
- Yield Routing Engine
- wallet infrastructure
- payment rails

Users do not interact with the engine directly.

Instead, they interact with product surfaces that expose outcomes generated by the engine, such as:

- stable savings balances
- yield accrual
- liquidity availability
- withdrawal readiness

The Stability Engine is therefore both:

- a treasury allocation system
- a capital safety control layer

---

# 3. Core Design Principle

The Stability Engine is governed by the following design principle:

## Stability Before Yield

The engine does not optimize for maximum returns.

It optimizes for:

1. preservation of purchasing power
2. capital safety
3. liquidity reliability
4. conservative yield generation

This principle is foundational and should not be violated by downstream implementation choices.

---

# 4. Core Responsibilities

The Stability Engine has five primary responsibilities.

## 4.1 Allocation

The engine determines how user capital is distributed across the defined allocation bands.

## 4.2 Rebalancing

The engine adjusts allocation positions when liquidity needs, protocol conditions, or risk exposure limits change.

## 4.3 Liquidity Preservation

The engine ensures that a portion of capital remains available for withdrawal and settlement operations.

## 4.4 Yield Routing Coordination

The engine determines how much capital may be deployed into external yield strategies and passes this capital to the Yield Routing Engine.

## 4.5 Risk Constraint Enforcement

The engine enforces caps and allocation limits designed to reduce concentration risk and preserve system safety.

---

# 5. Allocation Band Model

The Stability Engine distributes capital across three core allocation bands.

## 5.1 Stability Allocation

This band holds capital in stable assets intended to protect purchasing power.

Primary objective:

- capital preservation

Typical asset characteristics:

- low volatility
- high redeemability
- strong liquidity

Initial examples:

- USD-denominated stablecoins

## 5.2 Yield Allocation

This band deploys a controlled portion of capital into conservative yield strategies.

Primary objective:

- modest return generation without compromising system stability

Typical strategy characteristics:

- established protocols
- stable asset exposure
- transparent liquidity conditions

Initial examples:

- stablecoin lending strategies

## 5.3 Liquidity Allocation

This band holds capital reserved for user withdrawals, settlement operations, and short-term liquidity needs.

Primary objective:

- withdrawal reliability

Typical characteristics:

- immediately available
- minimal deployment risk
- operationally accessible

---

# 6. Allocation Logic

The engine uses a policy-driven allocation model.

At a high level, incoming user capital is handled as follows:

1. local currency is converted into supported stable assets
2. capital is assigned to the engine
3. the engine distributes capital across stability, yield, and liquidity allocations
4. the engine monitors state changes and rebalances when necessary

Allocation percentages are governed by separate allocation policy doctrine and may evolve over time.

The Stability Engine should not hardcode long-term allocation assumptions into product surfaces.

---

# 7. Rebalancing Logic

The engine may rebalance capital when one or more of the following conditions occur:

- withdrawal demand increases
- available liquidity drops below threshold
- protocol risk conditions deteriorate
- exposure caps are exceeded
- yield opportunities no longer satisfy policy constraints

Rebalancing should always prioritize:

1. restoring liquidity sufficiency
2. reducing unsafe concentration
3. preserving user capital
4. maintaining conservative yield exposure

---

# 8. Yield Coordination Model

The Stability Engine does not independently select speculative strategies.

Instead, it coordinates with the Yield Routing Engine.

The division of responsibility is as follows:

## Stability Engine

Responsible for:

- how much capital may be deployed
- allocation limits
- liquidity reservation
- safety constraints

## Yield Routing Engine

Responsible for:

- where approved yield capital is routed
- protocol evaluation
- strategy execution under engine constraints

This separation is intentional.

It ensures that capital safety logic remains distinct from strategy execution logic.

---

# 9. Risk Controls

The Stability Engine must enforce conservative risk controls.

These include:

## 9.1 Exposure Caps

No excessive concentration into any single external strategy or protocol.

## 9.2 Liquidity Floor

A minimum liquidity allocation must be maintained to support user withdrawal reliability.

## 9.3 Stable Asset Quality Constraints

Supported assets must satisfy quality and liquidity requirements before being included in allocation logic.

## 9.4 Rebalancing Guardrails

The engine must avoid unnecessary or excessive rebalancing that could introduce instability, execution costs, or operational risk.

---

# 10. System Invariants

The following invariants define the intended behaviour of the Stability Engine.

## Invariant 1

Capital preservation takes priority over yield optimization.

## Invariant 2

Liquidity availability must be preserved for withdrawals.

## Invariant 3

Yield allocation must remain policy-constrained and conservative.

## Invariant 4

No product surface may bypass engine allocation controls.

## Invariant 5

The engine is the canonical allocator of user capital within the Hedgr system.

These invariants should be treated as architectural doctrine rather than optional implementation preferences.

---

# 11. Product Surface Interaction

The Stability Engine powers all primary Hedgr product surfaces.

## Hedgr Wallet

Provides consumers with a simple savings experience while the engine manages capital behind the interface.

## Hedgr Pro

Allows treasury users to access managed stability, yield, and liquidity outcomes with greater visibility and future control features.

## Hedgr APIs

Exposes Stability Engine functionality to external platforms through controlled infrastructure endpoints.

All product surfaces depend on the Stability Engine as their common capital management layer.

---

# 12. Future Evolution

Over time, the Stability Engine may expand to support:

- multi-currency stability frameworks
- programmable hedging rules
- treasury policy customization
- institutional allocation controls
- proof and transparency systems

These expansions must preserve the engine’s foundational principle:

**stability first.**

---

# 13. Strategic Importance

The Hedgr Stability Engine is the core differentiator of the Hedgr platform.

Wallets can be replicated.
User interfaces can be redesigned.
Payment rails can be swapped.

The Stability Engine is harder to replace because it represents the logic layer that transforms Hedgr from a wallet product into a programmable financial stability system.

For this reason, the Stability Engine should be treated as:

- core doctrine
- strategic intellectual property
- primary architecture moat
