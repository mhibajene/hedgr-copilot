## 4. Why a cue-range note is needed

The Family C micro-test produced enough signal to move beyond discussing only `C1 / C2 / C3` as variants and to define the acceptable cue envelope inside the active family.

This note is needed to:

- preserve the value of `C1` as the calm floor
- preserve the value of `C3` as the upper-bound failure detector
- define where `C2` sits as the likely acceptable centerline range
- prevent future Family C fixtures from drifting into B-like precision or busyness

This is governance guidance only, not implementation direction.

---

## 5. Cue-range principles

### 5.1 Cue remains secondary

The structural cue stays subordinate to row clarity.

### 5.2 Cue remains interpretive

The cue may suggest structure, but must not imply exactness, live state, or ledger truth.

### 5.3 Cue remains calm

The cue must not raise visual temperature or turn allocation into a mini-instrument.

### 5.4 Cue remains hierarchy-safe

The cue must not pull the allocation block upward into hero-like salience.

### 5.5 Cue range is bounded by restraint

The acceptable range is defined as enough to help, not enough to impress.

---

## 6. Acceptable cue range

Inside Family C, the acceptable cue range sits closer to `C2` than `C3`.

It should retain more structure than `C1` while preserving row-first clarity. It should remain modest, non-heroic, and clearly interpretive. It should improve grasp without feeling measured or live.

---

## 7. Lower-bound cue risk

When Family C cueing becomes too weak, drift risk includes:

- collapse toward A-like passivity
- insufficient structural gain
- loss of the reason Family C was selected as the active family
- cue so recessive that it stops contributing meaningful distribution read

---

## 8. Upper-bound cue risk

When Family C cueing becomes too strong, drift risk includes:

- B-like precision implication
- chart-adjacent feel
- mid-layer busyness
- hierarchy creep
- visual over-assertion

`C3` was useful mainly to expose this boundary, not to normalize it.

---

## 9. Cue traits to preserve

- row-first readability
- modest secondary reinforcement
- calm structural assistance
- low visual temperature
- subordinate hierarchy
- non-exact interpretive read

---

## 10. Cue traits to avoid

- exact-looking segmentation
- chart-like emphasis
- visually live cues
- over-weighted contrast
- decorative structural sophistication
- anything that makes allocation feel more active than informational

---

## 11. Working cue-range signal

- `C1` remains the lower-bound calm floor.
- `C2` represents the likely acceptable Family C cue range.
- `C3` remains the upper-bound failure detector.
- Future Family C fixtures should screen against this envelope before any broader refinement continues.

This remains a working critique signal, not approved direction.

## 13. Working conclusion

Family C refinement should now be governed by cue-range discipline rather than by repeated broad comparison.

**Inside Family C, the right cue range is the one that strengthens structural understanding without making the allocation layer feel more exact, more active, or more important than it is.**
