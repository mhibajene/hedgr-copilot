## 1. Purpose

This document provides a repeatable critique instrument for evaluating the retail UI concepts described in:

- `HEDGR_RETAIL_UI_EXPLORATION_SYNTHESIS.md`
- `HEDGR_RETAIL_UI_PAPER_EXPLORATION_MATRIX.md`
- `HEDGR_RETAIL_UI_SCREEN_ANATOMY_COMPARISON.md`
- `HEDGR_RETAIL_UI_LOW_FIDELITY_WIRE_DESCRIPTIONS.md`

It exists to:

1. create a shared critique language
2. compare concepts consistently
3. reduce aesthetic or preference-driven drift
4. preserve doctrine-safe evaluation boundaries

This document does **not**:

- authorize implementation
- create sequencing authority
- approve a concept direction
- define product acceptance criteria
- reinterpret `docs/ops/HEDGR_STATUS.md`

This is a paper critique artifact only.

---

## 2. Authority boundary

This scorecard must be used under current repo truth:

1. Stability Engine retail presentation remains read-only and informational
2. allocation is informational, not accounting truth
3. no critique should reward execution semantics or hidden operational authority
4. no critique should assume approved Activity-surface expansion
5. no score in this document should be treated as a governance decision

This is an evaluation aid, not a decision log.

---

## 3. How to use this scorecard

Apply the same criteria to each concept.

The goal is not to find the most expressive concept.  
The goal is to identify which concept is most disciplined under current Hedgr doctrine.

Use a simple 1–5 scale for each criterion:

- **1** = weak / misaligned
- **2** = materially strained
- **3** = acceptable but unresolved
- **4** = strong
- **5** = very strong within current constraints

Scores are only useful when paired with short written rationale.

Do not treat totals as automatic decisions.  
A lower total with fewer doctrine risks may still be preferable to a higher total with structural tension.

## 4. Core critique criteria

### 4.1 Money-feel

Question: does the concept feel like real money quickly, without feeling like a crypto product or system console?

Focus:
- immediate financial familiarity
- balance anchoring
- non-crypto top-layer posture

### 4.2 Doctrine fit

Question: does the concept remain faithful to Hedgr’s read-only, informational, and structurally truthful posture?

Focus:
- no execution semantics
- no hidden-model authority
- no accounting-truth drift
- visible but bounded protection logic

### 4.3 Hierarchy clarity

Question: is the order of information easy to follow, with the right things prominent first?

Focus:
- portfolio value first
- stability status placement
- controlled sequencing of actions, activity, and trust detail

### 4.4 Trust legibility

Question: does the concept make protection feel understandable rather than abstract?

Focus:
- clarity of status
- visible trust signals
- understandable structural protection
- no mystical disclosure feel

### 4.5 Activity restraint

Question: does the concept use activity or money-reality surfaces helpfully without implying broader IA expansion?

Focus:
- scope discipline
- activity as orientation or trust surface only
- avoidance of dashboard sprawl

### 4.6 Allocation visibility

Question: does the concept make structured protection visible enough without creating shadow accounting or top-layer clutter?

Focus:
- visibility of protection logic
- disciplined placement
- no overstatement of precision or authority

### 4.7 Disclosure clarity

Question: does the deeper layer feel clarifying, optional, and bounded?

Focus:
- no score language
- no threshold-as-authority cues
- no hidden runtime truth feeling
- explanation without mystique

### 4.8 Calmness and tone

Question: does the concept remain calm, serious, and non-gamified?

Focus:
- no hype
- no urgency theater
- no celebratory or competitive feel
- institutional restraint without coldness

### 4.9 Hedgr distinctiveness

Question: does the concept still feel unmistakably Hedgr rather than a generic wallet or PFM dashboard?

Focus:
- protected money posture
- visible structural discipline
- differentiated trust surface

## 7. Comparative readout prompts

After scoring all three concepts, answer these questions in writing:

1. Which concept feels most like real money the fastest?
2. Which concept preserves doctrine best?
3. Which concept has the clearest hierarchy?
4. Which concept makes protection easiest to understand?
5. Which concept introduces the most scope-creep risk?
6. Which concept is the best current centerline for further critique?
7. What would need to change before any concept could be considered governance-ready for future review?

These prompts matter more than raw totals.

---

## 8. Interpretation guardrails

When using this scorecard:

- do not reward novelty for its own sake
- do not reward density as a proxy for sophistication
- do not reward simplification that conceals structural truth
- do not treat stronger activity presence as automatically better
- do not treat higher totals as automatic winners
- do not convert critique outcomes into implied roadmap authority

A concept may score well aesthetically and still fail Hedgr doctrine.  
A concept may score modestly but be the safest centerline for future work.

---

## 9. Open questions carried through critique

The scorecard does not resolve these questions automatically:

1. how much allocation detail belongs above the fold?
2. whether activity should read as recent activity or broader money reality
3. whether actions should always precede activity in the centerline
4. what should replace or preserve `Advanced Details`
5. where the warmth ceiling should sit once structure is stable

These remain governance-sensitive questions.

---

## 10. Closing position

This scorecard is meant to sharpen judgment, not flatten it into arithmetic.

The right concept will not be the most decorative or the most detailed.

It will be the one that best makes Hedgr feel:

- calm
- legible
- trustworthy
- structurally honest
- financially native without becoming generic
